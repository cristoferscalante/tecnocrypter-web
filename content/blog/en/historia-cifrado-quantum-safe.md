# History of encryption: from the Caesar cipher to quantum-safe

The need to protect information is as old as writing itself. From simple methods like the **Caesar cipher** to complex algorithms resistant to quantum computers, encryption has evolved with humanity.

## First methods

### Caesar Cipher
One of the first documented encryption methods, the Caesar cipher, was used by Julius Caesar to communicate with his generals. This simple method involved moving each letter of the message a fixed number of positions in the alphabet. Despite its simplicity, it was effective in a time where literacy was limited.

### Enigma
Centuries later, during World War II, the Enigma machine represented a qualitative leap in the complexity of encryption. Used by the Nazis for military communications, Enigma used mechanical rotors to create a polyalphabetic cipher that changed with each keystroke. The Allies' cracking of Enigma, led by Alan Turing, not only changed the course of the war, but laid the foundation for modern computing.

## Modern encryption

### RSA (1977)
The real turning point came in 1977 with the introduction of the RSA algorithm, developed by Rivest, Shamir and Adleman. RSA inaugurated the era of public key cryptography, allowing the secure exchange of information without the need to first share a secret key. This revolutionary advance made electronic commerce and secure communications on the Internet possible.

### AES (2001)
In 2001, the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) became the symmetric encryption standard adopted by the United States government. With key lengths of 128, 192, or 256 bits, AES offers a level of security that, with today's technology, would take millions of years to crack using brute force. Today, AES protects classified information and is used in countless commercial applications.

## The quantum challenge

Quantum computing represents both a threat and an opportunity for cryptography. Quantum computers, taking advantage of principles of quantum mechanics, could solve in hours problems that would take classical computers millions of years, such as the factorization of large numbers on which RSA is based.

Faced with this threat, **post-quantum cryptography** develops algorithms resistant to quantum attacks. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has already selected candidate algorithms for standardization, such as CRYSTALS-Kyber for key exchange and CRYSTALS-Dilithium for digital signatures.

## Conclusion

With each technological advance, encryption becomes more complex, but also more necessary. The history of encryption is a reflection of the constant race between protection and violation of information. From Julius Caesar's secret messages to quantum computing-resistant algorithms, the goal remains the same: ensure that only legitimate recipients can access information.

Knowing this history not only allows us to appreciate human ingenuity throughout the centuries, but also to better understand current and future challenges in protecting our digital privacy.
